Honors Faculty

Classes are led by world-renowned research faculty and leading industry experts who provide individual guidance and mentorship to students.

Calvin Lin

Calvin Lin is a University Distinguished Teaching Professor with research interests in computer architecture, compilers, and security. In addition to his technical research, he has led UT's NSF-funded effort to improve high school CS curricula. He is also the Director of the Turing Scholars Honors Program and the longtime coach of UT's Men's Ultimate Frisbee team. He has been awarded the Jean Holloway Award for Excellence in Teaching, one of the university's highest honors for teaching excellence. Dr. Lin received his BS from Princeton and his PhD from the University of Washington.

Honors courses: C S 314H Data Structures and Algorithms, C S 178H Intro to Research

Işil Dillig

Işil Dillig is an associate professor of computer science. Her main research areas are programming languages and formal methods (program verification and synthesis), but her research also touches on security, databases, and artificial intelligence. Dr. Dillig obtained all her degrees (BS, MS, PhD) from Stanford University, and she is also a Sloan Fellow as as well as an NSF CAREER award recipient. Her research has been recognized by numerous awards, including best paper awards at PLDI, OOPSLA, and ETAPS, and a CACM research highlight. Dr. Dillig has served as co-chair of CAV, the premier conference in Formal Methods, and she has served on program committees of most major programming languages and formal methods conferences.

Honors courses: C S 311H Discrete Math

Ahmed Gheith

Ahmed Gheith is an adjunct assistant professor of computer science. His interests are in research and advanced development of computer systems with an emphasis on computer architecture, operating systems, language run-times, and system middleware. He has a PhD from Georgia Tech, an MA from The Ohio State University, and a BSc from Alexandria University in Egypt.

Honors courses: C S 429H Computer Organization and Architecture, C S 439H Principles of Computer Systems

Eric Price

Eric Price is an assistant professor of computer science. His undergraduate and graduate education was at MIT, where Piotr Indyk was his advisor. After graduating in 2013, he was a postdoc at the Simons Institute in Berkeley and at the IBM Almaden Research Center before arriving at UT in Fall 2014.

Honors courses: C S 331H Algorithms and Complexity

Vijaya Ramachandran

Vijaya Ramachandran is the William Blakemore II Regents Professor in Computer Science. Her research interests are in theoretical computer science in the areas of algorithm design and analysis, data structures, graph theory and algorithms, and parallel computation. Dr. Ramachandran received her Ph.D. from Princeton University.

Honors courses: C S 331H Algorithms and Complexity

Chris Rossbach

Chris Rossbach is an assistant professor of computer science, a senior affiliated researcher at VMware Research Group, and a former Member of Microsoft Research Silicon Valley. His research is in operating systems, virtualization, accelerators, and emerging architecture. He received his PhD from UT Austin and his BS from Stanford.

Honors courses: C S 378H Concurrency

Scott Aaronson

Scott Aaronson is the David J. Bruton Centennial Professor of Computer Science at the University of Texas at Austin and director of its Quantum Information Center. Prior to joining UT, he taught for nine years at MIT. His research interests center around the capabilities and limits of quantum computers, and computational complexity theory more generally.

Honors courses: C S 358H Introduction to Quantum Information Science

Hovav Shacham

Hovav Shacham joined the University of Texas at Austin in 2018. His research interests are in applied cryptography, systems security, privacy-enhancing technologies, and technology policy. Shacham was a student at Stanford and a postdoctoral fellow at the Weizmann Institute. From 2007 to 2018, he was on the faculty at the University of California, San Diego. He received the 2017 ACM CCS Test-of-Time Award for his 2007 paper that introduced return-oriented programming. Shacham took part in California’s 2007 “Top-to-Bottom” voting systems review and served on the advisory committee for California’s 2011-13 post-election risk-limiting audit pilot program. His work has been cited by the Federal Trade Commission, the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, and the RAND Corporation.

Honors courses: C S 378H Network Security

Kristen Grauman

Kristen Grauman is a Full Professor in the Department of Computer Science where she leads the UT Computer Vision Group. Her research is in computer vision and machine learning. She is a Fellow of AAAI, an Alfred P. Sloan Research Fellow, and a recipient of the Presidential Early Career Award for Scientists and Engineers, the 2013 Computers and Thought Award, and several best paper awards. Prof. Grauman serves as Associate Editor-in-Chief for the IEEE Transactions on Pattern Analysis and Machine Intelligence. She was elected to the Academy of Distinguished Teachers in 2017, and received her B.A. from Boston College and her Ph.D. from MIT.

Honors courses: C S 378H Machine Learning and Vision

Peter Stone

Peter Stone is the David Bruton Jr. Centennial Professor of Computer Science and founded and directs the Learning Agents Research Group (LARG) within the Artificial Intelligence Laboratory. He is also the Executive Director of Sony AI America and an Alfred P. Sloan Research Fellow, Guggenheim Fellow, AAAI Fellow, and Fulbright Scholar. His main area of research is AI, focusing on machine learning, multiagent systems, and robotics.

Honors courses: C S 343H Artificial Intelligence

Scott Niekum

Scott Niekum is an assistant professor and the director of the Personal Autonomous Robotics Lab (PeARL) in the Department of Computer Science. He is also the recipient of the NSF CAREER Award, the AFOSR Young Investigator Award, and the UT Austin CNS Teaching Excellence Award. His research focuses on machine learning and robotics, including topics such as imitation learning, reinforcement learning, probabilistic safety, manipulation, and human-robot interaction.

Honors courses: C S 343H Artificial Intelligence

Adam Klivans

Adam Klivans is a full professor in the Department of Computer Science. His research interests lie in machine learning and theoretical computer science, in particular, Learning Theory, Computational Complexity, Pseudorandomness, Limit Theorems, and Gaussian Space. He also serves on the editorial board for the Theory of Computing and Machine Learning Journal. Adam Klivans will also be directing the newly established campus wide Machine Learning Laboratory, the winner of the NSF AI institute competition.

Honors courses: C S 378H Introduction to Data Mining/Machine Learning Honors
